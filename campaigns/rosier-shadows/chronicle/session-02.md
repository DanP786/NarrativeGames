# Session 02 — 10 May 2026 (real-world)

## Scene 1 — The Hogwarts Express, late afternoon (1 Sep 1998)
After burning the letter, Cass returned to the carriage and let the long middle of the journey wash over her — Daphne and Tracey carrying the surface conversation, Pansy reading without reading, Astoria asleep against the glass with her shoulder warm against Cass's own. Cass used the silence to think: the brass key is real, the cipher note is real, the third word is a wall she cannot get over without Brookhouse. She decided to write him soon — soon, but not so soon it looks frantic. The harder problem is the custodian. *Will not remember her* is softer than *Obliviated;* her mother would have said the latter. A woman seen as a baby, perhaps. A nurse, a tenant, a friend of her mother's from before Argus. There may be a photograph at the Hall. Yule is the soonest she can look.

Light went yellow, then dark. The carriage lamps came up. They changed into school robes; the green and silver looked, Cass found, the same as it had always looked, and she was slightly relieved by that.

## Scene 2 — Hogsmeade station and the Thestral carriages (1 Sep 1998, ~19:00)
The Highland cold came through the carriage door with the smell of pine and stone. Daphne — true to her warning — moved them to the right of the platform exit; the Thestral carriages had been relocated, and a Hufflepuff prefect with a clipboard was attempting (and visibly failing) to bracket older students with younger years.

The Thestrals split the school. Older students who had been at the battle could see them — a Ravenclaw seventh-year with his palm flat on an invisible flank, a Hufflepuff girl who had not stopped quietly crying since she got off the train, a pair of Gryffindor sixth-years stroking the air at face-height. First-years could not, and asked their older siblings why the carriages were pulling themselves. Most of the Slytherin third- and fourth-years could not either, and the small relief in their faces was its own admission. Cass could not see. Neither could Astoria. The war had made a register of itself in the eyes of the school, and most of Slytherin was on the wrong side of it.

Cass walked past the front of the carriage without putting out a hand. She would not perform that — not in a queue full of people watching each other.

**Memorable line.** Astoria, against the glass, after the carriage moved off: *"I can't see them either."* Cass: *"I know."*

## Scene 3 — Arrival and the Sorting (1 Sep 1998, ~19:30)
At the front steps Daphne quietly dismissed Cass to her year — *"Cassie. Find your year."* — and split off with Pansy, Millicent and Tracey toward the eighth-year end. Astoria, after a half-step's hesitation, fell in beside Cass instead of following her sister. Daphne did not look back.

The Slytherin table had gaps in it. Three places, then a body, then four places, then two bodies leaning together — the dead, the imprisoned, the pulled-out, the simply not returned. The fifth-year stretch had a hole in the middle of it. Cass took the seat with the gap on her right, Astoria on her left. The other fifth-years registered her arrival with the small careful adjustment of expression that the post-war Slytherin table had clearly been practising all summer. No one said *welcome back.* No one said her name.

The first first-year Sorted into Slytherin was the small girl with the too-long hair and the pale eyes — the one who had nodded at Cass on the platform. The Hat took longer over her than over the others. The Slytherin table clapped a beat too quietly and a beat too late. The girl walked the bench and did not look at Cass as she passed; she sat four down between two seventh-years who did not move to make space and did not move to deny it.
