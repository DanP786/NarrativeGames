# Narrative

Princess Seraphina Caldwyn, third in line to the Rime Throne of Caldros, has refused suitors for four years. Her mother has set a deadline. The Hoarfrost Tourney opens at the capital, staged as a marriage market in everything but name. Sera is twenty. She has seven months.

## The first day of the Hoarfrost Tourney (14th of First-Frost)
On the morning of the opening procession, a small girl of the bakers' guild was pushed forward to give Sera a wreath of bay-and-berry with three winter roses bound in red thread — the same shape the wreath had taken when it was given to her mother on her own first procession as a peace-bride twenty-eight years before. Sera took the wreath, plucked one rose, returned it to the child, and hung the rest on her saddle's pommel for the realm to see. The crowd's noise broke for it. The wreath survived the day on Whitewater's saddle.

At the opening ceremony of the lists, the Queen contrived that her daughter would tie the green-and-silver of Caldros around the wrist of Prince Cassian Verrent of Aerinhal — the marriage-match Lysandra has been quietly preferring since spring. Sera tied it a fraction too tight. Cassian did not flinch. The matter was, after that, no longer privately discussed by anyone who had been in the pavilion. In the same hour, the personal envoy of **Aerwin Vornlin** — third claimant to the Ostvar succession, son of a younger Vornlin prince thought long dead in exile — was formally received in the lists. The Rime Court had not previously acknowledged his existence. Aelric did not close his face on the question; he opened it, in his way.

Sera crossed two seats to sit with her brother's wife Eira, for the first time in three years of marriage, and found in her sister-in-law a woman trained at the long-knife and the short-spear in the Frostmarch yards until she was twelve, who has not lifted a blade in three years and misses it, and who is privately lonely in the chamber her marriage has given her. The two women have agreed, in everything but speech, to know each other now. A Frostmarch friend will be summoned north.

That evening, in her mother's small sitting room, Sera confirmed for Lysandra what Lysandra had separately confirmed for herself: that the Crown Prince of Caldros loves Ser Caelum Vesper, and has for almost four years; that the Queen has known three of those four; that the not-doing has been her policy and is now Sera's also. Lysandra promised her daughter that nothing would be done to Caelum without Sera hearing it from the Queen herself, in person. She also said she would ask, before the tourney closed, whether Theodric can stop — and that the answer to that question is the question of what is to be done next.

Returning to her own rooms late, Sera went instead toward the east garret to warn Caelum. She was intercepted in the gallery by Cassian Verrent, who showed her a kind of patience the court has not had with her in four years — clean offers, refused implications, a precisely judged cover story when a footfall came up the cross-corridor. The footfall was Caelum. He walked past them where they stood against the wall and made one small dry sound to say *I have seen and I am not in this room.* He did not stop. At her door Sera laughed, for the first time in months, and named the colour of a cloak she will accept from Aerinhal — a red the colour of old wine, with silver-grey wool inside, no Verrent crest. She called Cassian by his first name in the corridor. He has not, this night, asked her any question whose answer he does not deserve.

She is, perhaps, not entirely the woman she was this morning.

## The remainder of the Hoarfrost Tourney (15th–23rd of First-Frost)

The mornings of those nine days were the work that decided the rest of Sera's life. She came down to her mother's small parlour on the 15th wearing the silver Caldwyn circlet at the seventh bell, dressed for war; she left it having confessed the cloak, having heard her mother say *thank you,* and having been kissed on the crown of her head while she sat cross-legged in a chair like a child. She went down to the Tilting Field two hours before the lists and walked the perimeter under a hood. She found Cassian Verrent at the corner of his gear tent teaching a fourteen-year-old squire how to straighten a buckle in silence. She walked into his tent, pulled the flap closed, and asked him what he wanted; he said *yes* and then took a long answer to mean it. She gave him her own handkerchief as a favour, and he wore it tied at his left wrist at the third hour against Marrick Ardenmark, who took the second pass as an excuse to aim at her hand, and Cassian saw the move coming in the last twenty yards and unhorsed him under the shield with a strike Daron afterwards recognised as Theodric's. The handkerchief survived. The Caldwyn court, watching, recalculated.

She went to her father's audience chamber on the 16th in the silver gown and the new cloak Holsbrand had made overnight — old-wine-red Solinhar wool, silver-grey Caldwyn-wool at hem and hood, a hammered silver leaf-pin at the throat, no crest. Her father read the Aerinhal letter twice, accepted the proposal, and added his own condition: at the close of the tourney, in nine days, she would travel south with the Aerinhal delegation, reside in the Verrent house for one month as her engagement, and be married in the Aerinhal cathedral of Verrentine by Aerinhal rite at the Feast of the Lesser Lights. *I do not want to lose you a fourth time, Sera-min. Three is enough.* He told her, at parting, that he had noticed the cloak.

The other nine days were what nine days look like when a court has reorganised itself around a new fact. Sera visited Cassian's tent every morning at half past the eighth — twenty-seven half-hours over the nine, at the brazier, with Hallin at the canopy in the small constructed fiction of chaperonage. They did not kiss across that window; both held to it. They spoke instead of Aerinhal in winter, of each other's bookshelves, of Cassian's sister, of the household she would keep when she was south, of the Verrent librarian's bad scholarship, of the dower-house grandmother at the western lake. The Aelthyn letter went out under the Queen's seal on the 16th and was signed by Eira beneath the Queen's name; Riannon Aelthyn, of the three sisters, was the one named, and her acceptance arrived from the Frostmarch by return courier on the 21st. Theodric came down to the field on the 16th, in the open, with Eira at his side, and spoke to Cassian — what was said between them is private; what the court read was the Crown Prince clapping the Verrent prince's shoulder publicly and keeping his hand there for a count of two. By the closing days Theodric and Eira were sitting close enough that the court noted, and the household began to redraw quietly around them. The Aerwin Vornlin question stood through the tourney without resolving; Aelric is holding.

Sera spent a day in the orchard with Daron, who told her that if Cassian ever made her cry he would go south by night and burn the Verrent third bedroom down. She rode out to the hawking field with Theodric and Eira on the 21st; Eira said *I had not realised, when this all began, that I had not yet started missing you.* She spent the evening of the 22nd in her mother's small parlour, in Aerinic the whole evening, and was given the small silver locket with the grain of cardamom in it that her own mother had given Lysandra on the morning she left Aerinhal at nineteen. She said her last goodbye to Caelum at her chamber door late that night. They embraced for the second time since their grandfather's funeral. *Sera-min. Go well.*

Cassian won the joust outright on the closing day — five passes against the Whitewater Hills champion Sir Aelin, not the outcome the field had bet on — and Aelric put the Hoarfrost chain over his head himself on the grass. At the closing feast that night in the Long Hall, the Aerinhal Ambassador Lord Vesper read the formal proposal from the King of Aerinhal in the inner-court Aerinic, and Aelric spoke the consent from the dais without a written reply. *The Princess Cassara of Caldros and the Prince Cassian Verrent of Aerinhal are betrothed, by my hand, in the sight of this Hall, and the sight of God, and the sight of our two houses, on this the closing of the Hoarfrost Tourney of the Year of the Frost-Rose. God keep them both. God keep both their countries.* The Hall lifted. Sera stood on the dais with her hand in her father's, then in Cassian's. Aelric, very quietly under the cheer, said *my child. My son.* Looking at his daughter.

She leaves Caldemere at first light. The wedding is in seven to eight weeks. The campaign that began with a wreath on the courtyard stones has, in nine days, become a campaign about what kind of woman she will be when she walks into a country her mother left at nineteen and has never been back to.
