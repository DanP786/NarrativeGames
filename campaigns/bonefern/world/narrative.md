# Narrative

Mira Holloway wakes on Day 1 of her life on Vethaal — upside-down, naked, slung over the shoulder of an alien who has carried her for two days through the canopy. Her last memory is a transfer corridor on the colony ship *Helia-Voss* and a face she could not place. The alien — **Korren**, of the Vahn-rii river-walkers — found her on moss, in the lower spread east of his tribe's camp, with no tracks leading to her, *"like a thing that had been put there."* Three other Vahn-rii scouting parties find three more women in the same territory the same day. None of the women know each other beyond the ship; none of the Vahn-rii know what to make of this; nobody has yet asked, aloud, *what kind of thing puts a small unarmed naked person on the moss in our land.*

Mira spends the first afternoon on Korren's shoulder, then on her feet in front of his chief, then under the matriarch Senna's care at the women's fire — washed with bite-leaf, dressed in an improvised hide wrap that barely covers her, fed. She does not speak. At the evening fire Korren claims her formally with the coded language of the path: *"I will speak for her, eat for her, and bring her in time to be seen."* The chief, **Thaal,** recognises the claim. The senior scout **Drenn** uses his standing to press the moss-question into the open record. Before Korren can answer, three more women are brought in — one fighting, one dissociated, one watchful — and Drenn's question is answered at scale. One of the watchful one recognises Mira from the *Helia-Voss.* Mira recognises her back. They exchange a look — *I see you* — and Korren turns her away to his lodge.

Alone with him for the first time, Mira speaks. Her first volunteered words are not for herself — they are about the other women. Korren answers her about each one and then notes, mildly, what she has not asked. She looks up at him at last and asks her question. He answers in body and word: he will keep her, speak for her, and tonight he will see her. He undresses her. He names the rule that governs everything else: he will not be inside her until the cave, days away on a path she has not been told the meaning of; *"the deep places see that first."* Then he claims everything else of her body. They give and take pleasure of each other and he comes on her without cleaning her after. He pulls her into the curve of him in his furs and she falls into a heavy sleep marked with him.

The story is now in motion. There is a cave somewhere ahead, and a path of days, and a thing called *being seen* that Mira does not yet understand. There are four women in this tribe's hands and two unaccounted for. There is a chief who will require answers in the morning and a senior scout who will not let the question go. And there is the small surgical scar at the base of Mira's neck that two pairs of Kael'tha fingers have touched today, and that none of them — including Mira — yet know is there.

It is night. The hum of the river-walker against her back follows her into sleep.

## Day 2 — the implant, the reveal, and the decision to run for the dark
By the second morning the made thing in Mira's neck is no longer a secret kept from her: Senna names it aloud in the chief's lodge, and in the women's lodge reasons past Mira's own translation-chip theory to the harder shape of it — that Mira was *made easy to keep,* by a hand that wanted her either loved into a tribe or set down in one as a quiet unwitting eye, and that the part of her that would know which was cut away. Senna offers Mira the first true alliance she has had on Vethaal and the bluntest counsel: Korren is a good man and that is not the same as safe; keep something of yourself even from him. Mira carries the structural truth back to Korren but holds the warning about him — gives him *we were left vulnerable so that you would take us,* and watches him refuse to flinch from it and turn it into possession instead: a thing made to be taken is a thing he need never give back.

The central question stops being abstract. Offered one narrow chance to refuse, Mira chooses the path — *"I would like to see these caves,"* said lightly, a momentous thing made small so she can carry it — knowing roughly what it binds and nothing of the spore beneath it, reaching for Korren of her own hand even as neither of them can say whether her gladness is hers or grown in her by the maker. And Korren, the possessive want sharpened by the fear of losing her to whatever placed her, makes the reckless choice Senna named that morning into a plan: he will walk her to the deep places **at nightfall tonight,** against the chief's refusal and before the matriarch's verdict, and be two days into the canopy before the camp finds the furs cold. The bid collides with the matriarch before it is an hour old: Senna comes to the lodge cord to finish the neck-exam that could forbid the caves to Korren's face — and she has already read the defiance on him. The day is no longer drifting toward an ending. It is a fuse to nightfall.

## Day 2, late — the verdict, the wall, and the run taken
Senna's exam answers the open hinge: the implant is **awake.** It warms not to Mira's speech but to Korren's nearness — a made thing in her neck that responds to the male who claims her. Senna forbids the caves while it is awake. Korren's answer is a blade — *cut it out* — and Senna refuses it to his face, placing the thing against the cord of Mira's spine where a blind cut means her legs or her life; the lethal risk does not move him, and it is Mira's quiet *we would lose our language, you could not ask me a single thing* that sets the knife down. Senna names the shape of him aloud from it: a male the threat of his woman's death cannot reach, but the threat of losing the talk between them can — *keep that; it will tell you what you are.* The matriarch offers the only sanctioned road: days of watching the implant, and if it proves no eye, she will open the rock herself, properly. Then she goes to set the verdict before Thaal, and Korren walks with her carrying a thing he will not name — and is refused it: the chief upholds Senna (the caves are shut), and will not grant Korren leave to take Mira fully before the rock, for that rule is not his to unmake. Korren returns doubly walled, the want sharpened past patience.

He comes back to a changed lodge — Mira has, in an hour alone and obedient to his *stay,* set the place to rights and made the camp's foul standing water clean with her own hands and a child's errand, the first thing on this world to answer to her. It moves him the wrong way and the right way at once: every proof that she is *more* than the frightened thing he found makes her, in his mouth, *worse to lose.* And so he will not decide the night over the top of her again. He lays the whole cost bare — defiance of chief and matriarch, no asking twice, a return that may not be survivable — admits he wants it for the worse reasons as much as the right ones, tells her the truth of the caves as his people hold it (no one knows what waits; you go to be judged by the dark, not to see), and names the rock for what it is to him: *a door I can close behind us,* the one place no far hand can lift her off the moss again. He makes her choose with her eyes open. She chooses. *We go.*

Mira spends the waiting hours close to him and lets herself want this — lets herself like the possession that Senna called a danger, because after a life as a uniform nobody looked at twice, being kept this fiercely reads to her as safety; she half-suspects the pull is the maker's design in her neck and finds she cannot make herself care. The one snag is **Suvi** — the watcher, the kin from the ship — who reaches across the whole camp through the child Eshen, in the open, using Mira's *true name* that no Vahn-rii was given, asking *how the bad water was made good, and only you.* Mira puts her off to a *morning* she does not intend to keep, choosing Korren and the dark over the hand her own kind has put out. Then, after nightfall, with the fires banked and his light dimmed, Korren leads her out past the cord and up into the canopy — *you will not lose me in this* — and the firelight falls away behind them. The forbidden run is no longer a plan. It is underway, in the dark, toward a door and the thing none of them know is grown in the rock.

## Days 3–5 — the journey, the river, and the claiming in the deep places
The run becomes a four-day crossing of the canopy, and the canopy charges interest on every step. Mira's body cannot keep Korren's pace; she walks until she fails and is carried and set down to walk again, and learns the shape of being kept by a thing that does not tire. He tells her the truths she'd rather not have — that the deep places are sleeps away, not hours; that he will not carry her the whole of it; that by now the camp has found the furs cold and Drenn is reading their trail — and then chooses to spend her in the daylight rather than be caught resting, trading her exhaustion against the tracker and letting her watch him make the trade. The relationship deepens in the walking. He holds his pre-cave rule against rising pressure each night, the cost louder every time — hardest of all the dawn she wakes sprawled and half-bared across him and undoes him without meaning to, and he holds it anyway: *not here, not even you.* She, for her part, stops rationing what she gives him in sleep, and says the quiet thing aloud — *I'm not used to someone so dedicated; I like it* — and lets herself be, openly now, a woman who wants to be wanted this completely. He answers the question of why *her* and not a woman of his own people with an honesty that has an ugliness in it he won't file off: a tribe woman is shared — mothers, brothers, old debts — and Mira came with no kin, no claim, nowhere on the whole world to stand that is not against him, and he wanted that more than he should. And she *hums back,* which no tribe woman has ever done; he calls it the dark's doing and does not know it is the thing in her neck.

At a high, fast, flood-brown river the story hands Mira the one kind of problem she was built for, and she takes it cleanly: she reads the water like a failing life-support system, refuses the loud deep throat of it, and calls the safe braided crossing — float the gear, a hand-line and not a leash, caution over speed because *being caught is better than dead.* The choice costs them an hour they cannot spare and gives it to Drenn, and Korren pays it without complaint because her hands were right. It is the second time on this world her competence has answered to her, and the first that Korren has seen it save them rather than merely impress him; every such proof, in his mouth, only makes her *worse to lose.*

The country goes strange as they near the rock — older forest, luminous moss breathing brighter as they pass, Mira's hum no longer an echo but a deep pull up out of the ground, and the surgical scar at her nape waking to a warmth that is almost pressure. Korren reads it as the deep places beginning to *see* them, awe winning out over dread, and promises her the after: once the rock has judged and she is wholly his, the warm dark and a sleep as long as her body wants, *like a kept thing.* At the threshold she asks how they will know if they are bonded, and he tells her the rock does not lie — a false pairing gets nothing and goes home unclaimed; a true one, the light rises and the two hums become one. He is certain of theirs. He admits he has walked two women into this dark before and the dark gave nothing back either time — *I did not know then what the nothing meant* — and her flat, jealous *I will hear more of those women, after* only pleases him. Then she steps over the threshold of her own will.

In the green dark the rule he carried four days and nights finally breaks, and he claims her fully, and she meets it unashamed and choosing — and at the height of it the thing the whole campaign has been walking toward arrives. The scar floods, the moss-light surges white, and for one heartbeat the deep places are not a place but a presence — vast, patient, aware — reaching up through the dead machine in her skull and finding it awake. To Korren it is the simplest joy of his life: the rock has woken, the bond is true, there is no hand anywhere now that takes her back. To Mira, underneath the pleasure, a colder knowing she cannot yet name: that was *more* than a man and a woman in a cave; something reached into her and took, and only she half-felt it. The prohibition that walled the caves was, all along, the one thing accidentally shielding her from this — and she ran to it gladly, and it has now happened. The bond his people built a god out of is, in her and only in her, a thing with spores, and it is no longer ahead of them. It is done.

## Day 5 → ~Day 9 — the intrusion, the second bonding, and the reckoning brought home
The promised after lasts an hour. Drenn tracks the run all the way into the sacred caves and arrives with the last two women — **Ada Okonkwo** and **Mari Quintero**, gathered off the trail — and all six are accounted for at last. Then the engine does the one thing that turns Mira's private horror into a thing the world can see: it takes Drenn. The deep places bond the senior scout, helplessly and against his will, to the youngest woman, and Mira watches from the outside the exact event that happened inside her own skull hours before — the spore reaching through a second nape-implant, Mari humming back without knowing how. The bond is no longer a thing that happened once to one woman in the dark. It propagates, through the made-things in their necks, to whomever the cave can reach. When Drenn — a man who has governed himself his whole life — confesses *"I do not know what this is; the dark is meant to be asked, it does not ask,"* and turns his suspicion on the women (*what did you bring onto this rock*), Korren saves them all by weaponising his own people's faith: name it a sickness and you name yourself sick and the cave a liar; or call it the gift and stop fighting it. It defuses the contamination fear, buries Mira's secret deeper, and binds Korren personally to the lie that the dark chooses freely — but it also hands Drenn *permission* to stop resisting, and the older man settles into ownership of Mari.

The session's real weight falls on the central question. Tending the new women at a stream, Mira gives them the truth in their own tongue — *it's the flora, it's biology, it isn't you* — and then, asked by Ada for the rules and a way out, answers with the thing that frightens her most: *"I gave in to it. I like Korren. He makes me feel safe. I have him,"* and counsels terrified Mari that maybe Drenn could be that for her too. Ada, the clear-eyed engineer, names it without mercy — *that's the thing talking; it gets in and then it makes you its advocate* — and Mira cannot prove her wrong, because she meant every word and still cannot find the line where the implant ends and she begins. The kin-hand she might have taken is fractured: Ada will not be talked into surrender, and reads Mira now as compromised. Mira's answer to all of it is to retreat — into silence, into deference, into the curve of Korren's arm in front of the whole camp, the exact picture Ada drew of a woman who came home to the thing that took her.

That retreat carries her through the reckoning. The party comes down to camp two women heavier than it left, and the tribe counts in fear; Thaal demands his answer, Korren gives a good one, and **Senna** — not listening to the men — finds the made-thing in Mari's neck, twin of Mira's, and reframes the whole sacred order in front of the chief: the women were *made,* on purpose, by a hand that meant for this to happen — *the deep places', or something wearing the deep places like a skin.* She is closing on the truth from inside her own frame, and she has now felt it on two of them. Exposed (*she has known since I found hers*), Mira stays silent and eyes-down; Senna reads it not as guilt but as a woman *taught not to speak,* and pities her, which is worse. Korren ends the interrogation by claiming Mira out of it — *she is kept* — and Thaal tables the whole tangle until morning, then claims **Ada** for himself with a hand at her neck: a chief's plain power, no spore, no bond, the most resistant woman handed to the most powerful man, who is now too entangled to judge Korren cleanly. Two of the three arbiters of the reckoning are now mated or claiming. Ada's parting look to Mira is level and final: *you gave in; I'm not going to; watch me.*

The session closes alone, in Korren's lodge, on the one beat that complicates the slide. He asks her, gently and privately, what is in her neck and what she knows — and Mira gives him the first wholly unmanaged truth she has spoken to anyone since the moss: *I don't know.* It is true. She holds back only her guesses, a single door left shut, not a lie. He searches her face for a seam, finds none, and chooses to carry the not-knowing with her rather than against her — *I can carry not-knowing; I cannot carry you afraid in my own lodge* — and holds her, not to claim. And the relief that closes over her is the whole campaign in one sensation: she is not afraid here, and she cannot tell, anymore, whether that is love or the thing grown in her neck, and she is too tired tonight to grieve the difference. The reckoning waits for morning. So does the question of what Senna will find when she opens the new women, and whether Ada's *watch me* becomes the first crack in a world built on appetite and the dark.
